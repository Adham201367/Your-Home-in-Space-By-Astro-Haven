What is the International Space Station?

  • It is the largest inhabited space station in history and serves as a permanent space laboratory in low Earth orbit (at an altitude of approximately 400 km).

Project participants:

  • NASA (United States)
  • Roscosmos (Russia)
  • ESA (Europe)
  • JAXA (Japan)
  • CSA (Canada)

Its tasks and importance:

  • Scientific laboratory: Conducting hundreds of experiments in a zero-gravity environment in fields such as medicine, biology, physics, and materials science.
  • International cooperation: Represents a unique model of scientific and technological partnership between different countries.
  • Technology development: Helped invent air and water recycling systems, life support, and robots (such as Robonaut).
  • Earth and space monitoring: Used to monitor climate, natural disasters, and meteorites, as well as to study cosmic radiation.
  • Preparation for future missions: Studying the effects of space on the human body to prepare astronauts for long missions such as Mars.

Advantages:

  • A unique scientific platform in an environment that cannot be replicated on Earth.
  • The first permanent human habitat in space.
  • An opportunity to exchange expertise and cooperate with countries around the world.
  • Testing technologies that will be used in deep space exploration.

Disadvantages:

  • High cost: exceeding $150 billion.
  • Technical risks: such as air leaks or malfunctions in complex systems.
  • Space debris risk: the possibility of the station being struck by small particles in orbit.
  • Difficult living conditions: cramped space, strict schedule, constant reliance on supplies from Earth.
